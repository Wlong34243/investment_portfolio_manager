# AMD — Investment Thesis

## Core Thesis (Smart Second-Source AI View)

I am owning AMD as the **only credible merchant alternative to Nvidia in AI accelerators**, plus an ongoing data center CPU share gainer versus a weakened Intel.[file:7] Upside is tied to successful adoption of MI300/MI325 GPUs by cost‑sensitive hyperscalers and continued EPYC CPU share gains, which together drive mix toward higher‑margin data center revenue.[file:7]

AMD is the archetypal “smart second‑source” play: hyperscalers want it to succeed so they are not captive to Nvidia’s pricing power.[file:7] The key question is whether AMD’s software stack (ROCm) and ecosystem support can get good enough for AMD to capture and hold 10–20% of the AI accelerator market over time; I’m underwriting “yes,” with execution risk acknowledged.[file:7]

## Entry Context & Valuation

Cost basis: $183.32.[file:7] At entry, AMD traded on a volatile multiple: punished when PC/gaming softened, supported by explosive expectations around data center and AI.[file:7]

- I am **not** buying a cheap turnaround; I’m paying for AMD to secure a durable foothold in AI accelerators and keep taking CPU share from Intel.[file:7]  
- The entry assumes hyperscalers follow through on second‑source intent with real dollars and that data center becomes an ever‑larger share of the pie.  

## Bull Case (Why Own AMD Next to NVDA)

AMD complements NVDA exposure as a **share‑taker and pricing valve** rather than another “must‑own monopoly.”[file:7]

- Nvidia alternative: MSFT, META and others want a second source for AI GPUs; MI300‑class hardware is competitive on performance and memory bandwidth, and can win by being slightly cheaper.[file:7]  
- Data center CPU: EPYC continues to take share from Intel in traditional servers, creating a profitable, structurally growing base that supports corporate margins.[file:7]  
- Margin expansion: Mix shift away from low‑margin consoles/PCs toward high‑margin data center CPUs/GPUs should structurally lift profitability.[file:7]  
- Software tailwinds: Open‑source frameworks (PyTorch, Triton, etc.) that abstract away hardware reduce CUDA lock‑in at the margin and improve AMD’s odds in accelerators.[file:7]  

## Key Risks & Behavioral Guardrails

AMD is higher beta and “squeezed in the middle,” so I need tighter guardrails.[file:7]

- Software gap: ROCm has historically lagged CUDA in usability and stability; if the ecosystem does not materially close this gap, hardware wins won’t scale.[file:7]  
- Squeezed position: AMD faces Nvidia on one side and hyperscalers’ in‑house ASICs on the other; failure on either front can cap upside.[file:7]  
- Legacy drag: PCs and consoles remain cyclical and can mask data center progress at the top line.[file:7]  

Behaviorally:
- I will tolerate a 30–35% drawdown if:  
  - Data center growth (CPU+GPU) remains strong, and  
  - Hyperscalers reaffirm second‑source commitments with design wins and revenue.[file:7]  
- I will avoid averaging down solely on “it’s cheap on PE” if AI GPU revenue stalls or ROCm adoption clearly lags.  

## Hard Exit / Major Trim Conditions

I will exit or materially cut if the **“smart second‑source” and share‑gain thesis breaks:**[file:7]

1. AI GPU revenue (MI300/MI325) clearly stalls vs. internal or market multi‑billion‑dollar targets, showing AMD cannot crack Nvidia’s software moat.[file:7]  
2. Intel executes a real turnaround in data center CPUs (e.g., Sierra Forest / Granite Rapids) and stops or reverses AMD’s share gains.[file:7]  
3. Hyperscalers pivot primarily to their own ASICs for internal workloads, relegating AMD to a small, low‑value niche in AI compute.  
4. A superior, more attractively priced AI accelerator or semiconductor compounder emerges, offering a better risk‑adjusted IRR.
