# AMD — Investment Thesis

## Style
GARP (Growth at a Reasonable Price) / The "Number Two" AI Play / Data Center Market Share Taker

## Core Thesis
Buying the only viable, merchant silicon alternative to Nvidia in the AI GPU market, while simultaneously capturing continuous data center CPU market share from a severely weakened Intel. Upside is tied to the successful adoption of the MI300/MI325 series GPUs by cost-conscious hyperscalers, and gross margin expansion driven by a heavier mix of data center revenue. AMD is the classic "smart second-source" play. The key question is whether their software stack (ROCm) can improve enough to break Nvidia's CUDA monopoly and capture 10-20% of the AI accelerator market. Judgment: yes.

## Entry Context
Cost basis: $183.32. At entry, AMD was trading at a volatile multiple—punished when PC/gaming markets showed weakness, but supported by explosive data center expectations. Buying at this level means believing that AMD will secure a meaningful, permanent foothold in the AI accelerator market as hyperscalers look to avoid total reliance on Nvidia's pricing power.

## Bull Case
- **The Nvidia Alternative:** Hyperscalers (Microsoft, Meta) desperately want a second source for AI GPUs to maintain pricing leverage. AMD's hardware (MI300 series) is highly competitive on raw performance and memory bandwidth; if they sell it slightly cheaper than Nvidia, demand is guaranteed.
- **Data Center CPU Dominance:** While AI GPUs get the headlines, AMD's EPYC processors continue to steadily eat Intel’s lunch in traditional data center servers. This provides a highly profitable, structurally growing baseline of revenue.
- **Margin Expansion:** As AMD’s revenue mix shifts away from lower-margin gaming consoles (Xbox/PlayStation) and volatile consumer PCs toward high-margin data center CPUs and GPUs, overall corporate profitability structurally increases.
- **Open-Source Software Tailwind:** The industry (led by Meta, Microsoft, and others) is heavily investing in open-source AI frameworks (like PyTorch and OpenAI's Triton) that abstract away the hardware layer, directly weakening Nvidia's CUDA software moat and benefiting AMD.

## Key Risks
- **The Software Gap (ROCm vs. CUDA):** AMD's ROCm software stack has historically been buggy and difficult to use compared to Nvidia's seamless CUDA ecosystem. If developers refuse to adopt it, the hardware performance won't matter.
- **Squeezed in the Middle:** AMD faces a brutal two-front war: Nvidia at the high end for merchant GPUs, and the hyperscalers' own custom silicon (ASICs) at the lower end for internal workloads.
- **Legacy Business Drag:** The client (PC) and gaming console segments are highly cyclical and currently sluggish, routinely dragging down overall quarterly top-line growth and masking data center success.

## Exit Conditions
- AMD’s AI GPU revenue (MI300/MI325 series) stalls or misses internal multi-billion dollar targets, proving they cannot break Nvidia's software moat.
- Intel successfully executes a massive turnaround in data center CPUs (e.g., Sierra Forest/Granite Rapids) and halts AMD's market share gains.
- A clearly superior alternative emerges in the AI accelerator space, relegating AMD to a distant third place.
