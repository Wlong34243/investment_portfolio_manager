# UNH — Investment Thesis

## Style
Boring Fundamentals

## Core Thesis
Buying a dominant, defensive healthcare compounder at a valuation that has
reset after a major drawdown. Upside is tied to earnings normalization, margin
recovery, and continued cash generation — not heroic growth assumptions.
UnitedHealth is a high-quality franchise priced like a damaged one. The key
question is whether the regulatory noise and operational issues are temporary
enough to justify owning it before the recovery is obvious. Judgment: yes.

## Entry Context
Cost basis: $296.97. Stock fell from near $650. At entry, UNH was trading around
15x trailing earnings — cheap versus its own historical multiple. The drawdown
was driven by DOJ scrutiny, political/regulatory overhang, and one-off
operational headwinds, not by a structural break in the business.

## Bull Case
- Scale and diversification: insurance franchise (UnitedHealthcare) plus Optum
  services gives multiple earnings engines and cross-selling advantages that
  most healthcare peers lack.
- Recovery setup: the stock is discounting a messy but temporary period.
  Recovery expected over 3–5 years as operational issues normalize.
- Valuation support: 15x trailing earnings at entry is cheap for a business
  with UNH's track record. Consensus analyst target ~$401, well above recent
  trading levels in the high-$200s.
- Defensive demand: healthcare spending is relatively resilient in weak macro
  environments — adds a lower-volatility compounding quality to the position.
- Cash return: dividend growth and strong free cash flow conversion support
  total return even if multiple expansion is slow.

## Key Risks
- Regulatory and legal overhang: DOJ scrutiny and political noise can keep the
  multiple compressed longer than expected. This is the primary thesis risk.
- Slower recovery: the path back is longer and riskier than it looked at entry.
  This is possible and is why position sizing stays disciplined.
- Execution risk: if margins don't normalize, the valuation case weakens fast.
