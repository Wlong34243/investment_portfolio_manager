# VEA — Investment Thesis

## Style
Passive Core / Developed International Diversification / Value

## Core Thesis
Buying the entire developed world outside of the United States. Upside is driven by a structural reversion to the mean in valuation: international stocks (Europe, Japan, Australia) trade at massive, historic discounts to US equities. VEA captures world-class, multinational compounders (Novo Nordisk, ASML, Toyota, Nestle) that are deeply entrenched in the global economy. The key question is whether a weakening US Dollar and corporate governance reforms in Japan/Europe can finally trigger a sustained period of international outperformance after a decade of US dominance. Judgment: yes, as a core diversification tool.

## Entry Context
Cost basis: ~$58.37 (Assumed). Buying VEA is the definition of a long-term, passive asset allocation decision. You are not timing a breakout; you are securing cheap exposure to global GDP growth with an attractive dividend yield, protecting your portfolio from the risk of the US market suffering a "lost decade" of flat returns due to its high current valuation.

## Bull Case
- **Historic Valuation Discount:** The gap between US P/E multiples and International P/E multiples is near all-time highs. If global growth normalizes, capital will naturally rotate out of expensive US tech and into cheap European and Japanese value stocks.
- **World-Class Compounders:** VEA is not just "slow European banks." It holds absolute global monopolies in pharmaceuticals (Novo Nordisk's Ozempic), semiconductor equipment (ASML), and luxury goods (LVMH) that are immune to regional borders.
- **Currency Tailwinds:** VEA is unhedged. If the US Dollar weakens from its historic highs—due to Fed rate cuts or expanding US debt—the foreign currencies held in VEA will appreciate, boosting the fund’s USD-translated returns.
- **Dividend Yield:** Developed international markets have a much stronger cultural emphasis on returning cash to shareholders via dividends than the US, providing a steady, high-yielding cash flow stream.

## Key Risks
- **The US Exceptionalism Trap:** If the US continues to completely monopolize the artificial intelligence boom, the US market may structurally deserve its massive premium, leaving VEA to chronically underperform for another decade.
- **European Stagnation:** The Eurozone frequently flirts with recession, hindered by heavy regulation, lack of a unified tech sector, and an ongoing energy/geopolitical crisis stemming from the war in Ukraine.
- **Strong Dollar Permanence:** If global instability persists, the US Dollar will remain the ultimate safe haven. A perpetually strong dollar will constantly erode the returns of VEA for a US-based investor.
