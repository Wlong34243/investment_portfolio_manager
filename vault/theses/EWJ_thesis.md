## Core Thesis
*(Note: The core thesis for EWJ is identical to BBJP. Both track Japanese equities, but EWJ is the older, massive flagship ETF run by iShares. Upside is driven by the end of deflation, rising wage growth, and the Tokyo Stock Exchange forcing cash-hoarding corporations to buy back stock and improve return on equity. EWJ serves as the premier, highly liquid vehicle to capture the Japanese value realization cycle and a structurally weak Yen. The key question is whether the Bank of Japan can thread the needle on interest rate normalization. Judgment: yes.

## Bull Case
- **Corporate Reform:** The relentless pressure from the Tokyo Stock Exchange to improve capital efficiency is working. Japanese companies are unlocking trapped value, raising dividends, and shrinking their float via buybacks.
- **Macro Inflection:** Japan has officially escaped its 30-year deflationary trap. Real wage growth is stimulating domestic consumption, creating a healthier, self-sustaining internal economy.
- **Supreme Liquidity:** As the flagship Japan ETF, EWJ boasts massive daily trading volume and a highly liquid options chain. This makes it the superior choice (over BBJP) if you actively trade covered calls, protective puts, or need to liquidate a massive position instantly without slippage.
- **Export Dominance:** A structurally weak Yen acts as a massive tailwind for the industrial, automotive, and technology exporters that heavily weight the top of the EWJ index.

## Key Risks
- **Bank of Japan Policy Error:** If the BOJ raises interest rates too aggressively to combat inflation, they risk crashing the Japanese bond market and instantly plunging the economy back into recession.
- **Currency Headwinds (USD/JPY):** EWJ is unhedged. If the Yen sharply appreciates against the US Dollar (e.g., if the US Federal Reserve slashes rates while the BOJ raises them), the USD-translated value of the ETF will drop significantly.
- **Expense Ratio Drag:** At 0.50%, EWJ is significantly more expensive to hold long-term than its competitors (like BBJP). Over a 10-year horizon, this fee drag will mechanically underperform a cheaper equivalent index.
