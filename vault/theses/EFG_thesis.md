# EFG — Investment Thesis

## Style
International Factor / Developed Market Growth

## Core Thesis
Buying a highly targeted basket of the fastest-growing, highest-quality companies in Europe, Japan, and Australia, while explicitly filtering out the slow-moving, value-trap banks and legacy energy companies that typically drag down broad international indices (like VEA). Upside is driven by the structural dominance of European luxury goods (LVMH, Hermes), world-class healthcare, and specialized industrial tech. EFG is a bet that international growth can rival US growth if you simply strip out the dead weight. The key question is whether these premium international compounders can justify their higher valuations in a sluggish Eurozone economy. Judgment: yes, as an international alpha-generator.

## Entry Context
Cost basis: ~$110 (Assumed). At this level, EFG trades at a premium multiple compared to broad international indexes, reflecting the high ROIC (Return on Invested Capital) and deep economic moats of its underlying holdings. Buying here means you are actively choosing to pay up for quality abroad rather than simply buying a cheap, passive index.

## Bull Case
- **Quality Over Quantity:** EFG purposefully screens for earnings growth. This means you skip the struggling, highly regulated European telecom and utility sectors, and instead heavily concentrate in global juggernauts like ASML, Novo Nordisk, SAP, and L'Oréal.
- **The Luxury Moat:** European luxury brands possess pricing power that is virtually unmatched globally. They sell Veblen goods to the ultra-wealthy, making them incredibly resilient to standard macroeconomic inflation or recessions.
- **Outperformance Potential:** By isolating the "growth" factor, EFG has historically outperformed broad international indexes (like EFA or VEA) over long time horizons, proving that filtering for quality works overseas just as well as it does in the US.
- **Diversified AI Exposure:** While the US dominates AI software, EFG captures the international hardware and logistics backbone of AI—specifically holding ASML (the monopoly on EUV lithography machines) and SAP (enterprise software transition).

## Key Risks
- **Higher Expense Ratio:** At 0.39%, EFG is significantly more expensive than standard Vanguard index funds. You are paying a premium for the factor-screening methodology, creating a higher hurdle rate to achieve alpha.
- **US Tech Dominance:** Even the best international growth companies struggle to match the sheer revenue scale and network effects of the American mega-cap tech monopolies (Microsoft, Apple, Google).
- **China Consumption:** The European luxury goods sector is heavily reliant on the Chinese consumer. A severe, prolonged deflationary depression in China will crush the earnings of LVMH, Hermes, and Richemont.

## Exit Conditions
- The "Growth" factor structurally breaks down globally, entering a multi-year period where value and high-yield stocks heavily outperform.
- A collapse in Chinese luxury spending permanently impairs the European consumer discretionary sector.
- You decide the 0.39% expense ratio is dragging down returns and opt to rotate the capital into a cheaper, broad-market international ETF.
